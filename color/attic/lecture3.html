Cone Cells

In a currently prevalent scientific model, color perception is effected by the stimulation of photoreceptor cells on the retina. One class of such cells are called cone cells, of which there are three types: L, M, and S. Their responsiveness to light of various ranges of wavelengths peak at approximately:
  • 564 - 580 nm (Long wavelength),
  • 534 - 545 nm (Medium),
  • 420 - 440 nm (Short),
respectively.

Normalized responsivity spectra of human cone cells, S, M, and L types (from Wikipedia.)
@sep In the Opponent Process Model, signals from the cone cells (and rod cells) go through another layer of processing, in which calculations based on the relative strengths of the signals give rise to our perception of hue, saturation/chroma and brightness/lightness. @sep In one proposed model, the opponent process filters signals from photoreceptor cells into three channels of opponent color sensations:
  • Red-Green (R-G)
  • Yellow-Blue (Y-B)
  • Black-White (the Achromatic response channel)

@sep Experimental data suggest that the sensation of red-versus-green is linked to a numerical value derived from photochemical stimuli. Depending on whether this number is positive, negative or zero, we "see" red, green or neither-red-nor-green, respectively.

Similarly, the color sensation of yellow-versus-blue is linked to another number derived from photochemical stimuli. We see yellow, blue or neither-yellow-nor-blue, depending on whether this number is positive, negative or zero, respectively.

An Opponent-Process Theory of Color Vision, L. Hurvich, D. Jameson
@sep It has been proposed that an S cone stimulus contributes positively to the red-green channel. Under this proposition, it is easy to see why spectral colors towards the violet end of the visible color spectrum appears reddish.

Moreover, this explains why, perceptually, it appears natural to wrap the visible hue spectrum into a color wheel, with magenta serving as the link between violet and red.

(Food for thought: there is no magenta on the rainbow.) @sep The opponent process model explains why even though the additive mixture of red with green gives yellow, we do not "perceive" yellow as such a mixture. Instead, we view yellow as a color which is neither red nor green.

@sep In an opponent process model, our perception of color is established via comparison. This may explain some of the color illusions which result when we place colors of different hues or lightness adjacent to each other, e.g. a neutral grey appearing pinkish next to a greenish blue color.

@sep It also explains the illusion of complementary afterimages, where, for example, someone having stared at a red color for a long time who then suddenly looks upon a white sheet might see a green color. @sep @sep @sep @sep

@sep The achromatic channel is most responsive to spectral light which lies in the green or yellowish-green range. This may explain why green and yellow spectral colors appear brighter than the rest.

Moreover, "saturation" may now be understood as a function of the "relative amounts of chromatic and achromatic responses." Hence,

At the two spectral extremes where the chromatic responses are large relative to the white response, the spectral saturation is high.

Color Constancy

In the context of trichromatic color theory, an object's color is dependent upon the the color of the light which illuminates it. For example, a red apple lit by a blue light should appear purple.

However, it appears that the brain has a built-in mechanism to filter out lighting conditions and interpret (unconsciously) the "true" color of an object.
